\[ \operatorname{Re} F\left( \frac{1}{2} + \sigma + i t_0 \right) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} u(t) \, P_\sigma(t - t_0) \, dt \geq 0 \]


The Poisson kernel is \( P_\sigma(\xi) = \dfrac{1}{\pi} \dfrac{\sigma}{\sigma^2 + \xi^2} \).


To arrive at this, note that \( \operatorname{Re} F \) is harmonic in \( \{ s \in \mathbb{C} : \operatorname{Re} s > 1/2 \} \) since \( F \) is analytic there. The representation formula then gives the equality. The inequality holds because \( u(t) \geq 0 \) almost everywhere and the kernel \( P_\sigma(t - t_0) > 0 \) for all \( t \), with the integral assumed to converge absolutely under the problem's conditions.

Here is a drop‑in LaTeX proof script that establishes the “outer cancellation on the boundary” (phase neutralization) exactly as stated and then concludes the replacement U↦U−ℜlog⁡OU\mapsto U-\Re\log O.
% Phase neutralization by an outer factor


% Setup and notation.
% We write s = \sigma + it and work in the right half-plane
%   \mathbb H_{1/2} := \{ s : \sigma > 1/2 \}.
% Gradients and area measure are taken with respect to (\sigma,t):
%   \nabla = (\partial_\sigma,\partial_t),\quad dA = d\sigma\,dt.


\noindent\textbf{Proposition (boundary phase from an outer factor equals an interior pairing).}
Let \(O\) be an outer function on \(\mathbb H_{1/2}=\{\sigma>1/2\}\) with boundary modulus
\(|O(1/2+it)|=e^{u(t)}\). Write
\[
\log O = U_O + i\,\Phi_O \quad\text{on }\mathbb H_{1/2},
\]
so \(U_O=\Re\log O\) is the Poisson extension of \(u\) and \(\Phi_O=\Arg O\) is its harmonic conjugate.
Let \(\psi\in C_c^\infty(\mathbb R)\) be a boundary test function, let \(V\psi\) denote the (half‑plane) Poisson
extension of \(\psi\) into \(\mathbb H_{1/2}\), and let \(\chi\in C_c^\infty([1/2,\infty))\) be a smooth cutoff with
\(\chi\equiv 1\) on \([1/2,1/2+\delta]\) and \(\chi\equiv0\) for \(\sigma\ge 1/2+2\delta\).
Adopting the \emph{inward} normal on \(\{\sigma=1/2\}\), one has
\[
\int_{\mathbb R}\psi(t)\,\partial_t\Phi_O(1/2,t)\,dt
\;=\;
\iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} \nabla U_O(\sigma,t)\cdot \nabla\!\big(\chi(\sigma)\,V\psi(\sigma,t)\big)\,dA
\;+\; \mathrm{Err}[U_O,\psi],
\]
where the error term is supported where \(\chi'\neq 0\) and admits the explicit representation
\[
\mathrm{Err}[U_O,\psi]
= -\iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} U_O(\sigma,t)\,\Delta\!\big(\chi(\sigma)\,V\psi(\sigma,t)\big)\,dA
= -\iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} U_O\!\left(\chi''\,V\psi + 2\chi'\,\partial_\sigma V\psi\right)\!dA.
\]
In particular, it obeys the same remainder control as in the base identity (uniformly in \(\psi\) and \(U_O\))
since it is localized to the fixed compact strip \(\{1/2+\delta\le \sigma\le 1/2+2\delta\}\).


\emph{Proof.}
Since \(U_O\) is harmonic and \(V\psi\) is harmonic, Green’s identity with inward normal \(n=+\partial_\sigma\) gives
\[
\iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}}\nabla U_O\cdot \nabla(\chi V\psi)\,dA
= \int_{\sigma=1/2} \psi(t)\,\partial_\sigma U_O(1/2,t)\,dt
\;-\;\iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} U_O\,\Delta(\chi V\psi)\,dA.
\]
By the Cauchy–Riemann equations for \(\log O=U_O+i\Phi_O\) on \((\sigma,t)\),
\(\partial_t\Phi_O=\partial_\sigma U_O\). Substituting this into the boundary term yields the displayed identity,
with the error written explicitly above. The support and bounds of \(\mathrm{Err}[U_O,\psi]\) follow from
\(\Delta(\chi V\psi)=\chi''\,V\psi+2\chi'\,\partial_\sigma V\psi\), which is supported where \(\chi'\neq 0\). \qed


\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Corollary (outer cancellation / phase neutralization).}
Suppose an identity of the form
\[
\int_{\mathbb R} \psi\,\partial_t \Phi = \iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} \nabla U\cdot \nabla(\chi V\psi)\,dA
\;+\;\mathrm{Err}[U,\psi]
\]
holds (with the same remainder control). Replace \(U\) by \(\widetilde U:=U-\Re\log O\) and the boundary phase by
\(\widetilde\Phi:=\Phi-\Arg O\). Then
\[
\int_{\mathbb R}\psi\,\partial_t \widetilde\Phi
= \iint_{\mathbb H_{1/2}} \nabla\widetilde U\cdot \nabla(\chi V\psi)\,dA
\;+\;\mathrm{Err}[\widetilde U,\psi],
\]
i.e. the outer factor cancels on the boundary phase side and only the interior energy of
\(\nabla(U-\Re\log O)\) remains.


\emph{Proof.}
Linearity gives
\[
\int \psi\,\partial_t \widetilde\Phi
= \Big(\int \psi\,\partial_t \Phi\Big) - \Big(\int \psi\,\partial_t \Arg O\Big)
= \iint \nabla U\cdot \nabla(\chi V\psi)\,dA - \iint \nabla(\Re\log O)\cdot \nabla(\chi V\psi)\,dA
+ \mathrm{Err}[U,\psi]-\mathrm{Err}[\Re\log O,\psi].
\]
Apply the Proposition to the \(\Arg O\) term to replace it by the corresponding interior pairing (with its error).
Grouping terms yields the stated identity with \(\widetilde U\); the remainder retains the same control because the
error operator is linear in \(U\) and supported where \(\chi'\neq 0\). \qed


\medskip
\noindent\emph{Remark on signs.}
We used the inward normal on \(\{\sigma=1/2\}\) so that \(\partial_n=\partial_\sigma\).
If one works with the outward normal, an overall minus appears on both sides and cancels in the corollary.

Here’s a clean, self‑contained solution. (With the normalization Pb(y)=1π bb2+y2P_b(y)=\tfrac{1}{\pi}\,\dfrac{b}{b^2+y^2} used below, the optimal constant is actually 1πarctan⁡2\tfrac{1}{\pi}\arctan 2; the 12πarctan⁡2\tfrac{1}{2\pi}\arctan 2 bound is valid but weaker.)
% Poisson plateau lower bound for a flat-top window
% Normalization: P_b(y) = (1/\pi) b/(b^2 + y^2).


\newcommand{\Pb}{P_b}
\newcommand{\one}{\mathbf{1}}


\paragraph{Claim.}
Let $\Pb(y)=\tfrac{1}{\pi}\,\dfrac{b}{b^2+y^2}$, $b>0$. Suppose $\psi\in C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$ is even, $0\le \psi\le 1$, $\psi\equiv 1$ on $[-1,1]$, and $\operatorname{supp}\psi\subset[-2,2]$.
Then
\[
c_0(\psi)\;:=\;\inf_{\substack{0<b\le 1\\ |x|\le 1}}\;(\Pb * \psi)(x)
\;\ge\;\frac{1}{\pi}\arctan 2
\;>\;0.
\]


\paragraph{Proof.}
Because $0\le \psi$ and $\psi\ge \one_{[-1,1]}$, and $\Pb\ge 0$, we have the pointwise lower bound
\[
(\Pb * \psi)(x)\;\ge\;(\Pb * \one_{[-1,1]})(x)\qquad\text{for all }x\in\mathbb{R},\ b>0.
\]
Thus it suffices to compute $F_b(x):=(\Pb * \one_{[-1,1]})(x)$ and minimize it on $0<b\le 1$, $|x|\le 1$.


A direct integration gives a closed form:
\[
F_b(x)
=\int_{-1}^{1}\frac{1}{\pi}\frac{b}{b^2+(x-t)^2}\,dt
=\frac{1}{\pi}\Big[\arctan\!\Big(\frac{t-x}{b}\Big)\Big]_{t=-1}^{t=1}
=\frac{1}{\pi}\!\left(\arctan\frac{1-x}{b}+\arctan\frac{1+x}{b}\right).
\]


Fix $b>0$. Since $\arctan$ is concave on $[0,\infty)$ and $x\mapsto \tfrac{1\pm x}{b}$ are affine, each term
$x\mapsto \arctan\!\big(\tfrac{1\pm x}{b}\big)$ is concave on $[-1,1]$, hence their sum $F_b$ is concave on $[-1,1]$.
A concave function attains its minimum over a compact interval at an endpoint, so
\[
\min_{|x|\le 1} F_b(x)=F_b(1)=\frac{1}{\pi}\arctan\!\Big(\frac{2}{b}\Big).
\]
Next, $b\mapsto \arctan\!\big(\tfrac{2}{b}\big)$ is strictly decreasing for $b>0$, so over $0<b\le 1$ the minimum occurs at $b=1$:
\[
\inf_{0<b\le 1,\;|x|\le 1} F_b(x)=F_1(1)=\frac{1}{\pi}\arctan 2.
\]
Combining with $(\Pb * \psi)\ge F_b$ gives
\[
c_0(\psi)\;\ge\;\frac{1}{\pi}\arctan 2\;>\;0,
\]
as claimed. \hfill$\square$


\paragraph{Remarks.}
1) The constant $\tfrac{1}{\pi}\arctan 2\approx 0.3524$ is sharp for the data $\psi=\one_{[-1,1]}$, achieved at $(x,b)=(\pm 1,1)$.
2) If one instead normalizes the Poisson kernel with a prefactor $\frac{1}{2\pi}$, the corresponding bound becomes $\tfrac{1}{2\pi}\arctan 2$.

Here is a self‑contained proof script (LaTeX) of the Whitney CR–Green pairing with scale‑invariant remainders, exactly in the form you requested.
% Whitney CR–Green pairing with scale-invariant remainders


\newcommand{\QQap}{Q(\alpha' I)}
\newcommand{\QQa}{Q(\alpha I)}


\textbf{Proposition (Whitney CR–Green pairing).}
Let $I\subset\mathbb{R}$ be an interval with center $t_0$ and length $L>0$, and
\[
  \QQap := I \times (0,\alpha' L]\subset\mathbb{R}\times(0,\infty).
\]
Assume $U$ is harmonic on $\QQap$, $\chi\in C^\infty_c(\QQap)$ satisfies $\chi\equiv 1$ on $\QQa$ and $\|\nabla\chi\|_{L^\infty}\lesssim L^{-1}$, and let $V_\psi$ be the Poisson extension of a fixed test $\psi_{L,t_0}$ supported in (a constant multiple of) $I$. Suppose $J$ is analytic in the upper half–plane with
\[
\log J = U + i W,
\]
and write $W'(t):=\partial_t W(t,0)$ for the boundary phase derivative. Then
\begin{equation}
\iint_{\QQap} \nabla U \cdot \nabla\!\big( \chi\, V_\psi \big)\, dt\, d\sigma
= \int_I \psi_{L,t_0}(t)\,(-W'(t))\,dt \;+\; R_{\mathrm{side}} + R_{\mathrm{top}},
\label{eq:pairing}
\end{equation}
where the remainder terms are the side and top boundary contributions
\[
R_{\mathrm{side}}+R_{\mathrm{top}}
:= \int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \chi\, V_\psi\, \partial_n U \, ds,
\]
and satisfy the scale–invariant bound
\begin{equation}
|R_{\mathrm{side}}| + |R_{\mathrm{top}}|
\;\lesssim\;
\Bigg(\iint_{\QQap} |\nabla U|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma\Bigg)^{\!1/2}
\cdot \mathcal{E}_\psi,
\qquad
\mathcal{E}_\psi \;\simeq\; \|\psi_{L,t_0}\|_{\dot H^{1/2}(\mathbb{R})},
\label{eq:remainder-bound}
\end{equation}
with an absolute implicit constant depending only on $\alpha,\alpha'$ and the fixed test template. In particular, for a normalized Poisson test, $\mathcal{E}_\psi$ is a universal constant (independent of $I,t_0,L$ and of $U$).


\textit{Proof.}
We work in coordinates $(t,\sigma)$ with $\sigma>0$ the vertical variable. Since $U$ is harmonic on $\QQap$, the divergence identity gives
\begin{equation}
\iint_{\QQap} \nabla U \cdot \nabla\!\big(\chi V_\psi\big)\, dt\, d\sigma
= \int_{\partial \QQap} \chi V_\psi\, \partial_n U \, ds,
\label{eq:green}
\end{equation}
because $\Delta U=0$ in $\QQap$. Split $\partial\QQap$ into the bottom edge $I\times\{0\}$ and the top/side boundary.


\smallskip
\emph{Bottom contribution.} On $I\times\{0\}$ the outward unit normal is $n=-\partial_\sigma$, i.e. $\partial_n=-\partial_\sigma$. Since $V_\psi(t,0)=\psi_{L,t_0}(t)$ and the Cauchy–Riemann equations for $\log J=U+iW$ yield
\[
\partial_\sigma U(t,0) = -\,\partial_t W(t,0) = -\,W'(t),
\]
we get
\[
\int_{I\times\{0\}} \chi V_\psi\, \partial_n U\, ds
= \int_I \psi_{L,t_0}(t)\, \big(-W'(t)\big)\, dt.
\]
This is the main pairing term on the right side of \eqref{eq:pairing}.


\smallskip
\emph{Side/top remainder.} Define
\[
R_{\mathrm{side}}+R_{\mathrm{top}} := \int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \chi V_\psi\, \partial_n U \, ds.
\]
By Cauchy–Schwarz with the scale–neutral weights $\sigma$ and $\sigma^{-1}$,
\begin{equation}
|R_{\mathrm{side}}|+|R_{\mathrm{top}}|
\le
\Bigg(\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma\, |\partial_n U|^2\, ds \Bigg)^{\!1/2}
\Bigg(\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma^{-1}\, |\chi V_\psi|^2\, ds\Bigg)^{\!1/2}.
\label{eq:CS}
\end{equation}


\emph{First factor (Dirichlet energy control).}
A Whitney–Carleson trace estimate on the box yields
\begin{equation}
\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma\, |\partial_n U|^2\, ds
\;\lesssim\; \iint_{\QQap} |\nabla U|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma.
\label{eq:trace-U}
\end{equation}
This follows by covering $\QQap$ with Whitney rectangles of size comparable to the local height $\sigma$, noting each boundary element is adjacent to a bounded number of such rectangles, and summing the interior $L^2$ control of $\nabla U$ over that cover.


\emph{Second factor (Poisson test energy).}
Since $\chi\equiv 1$ on $\QQa$ and $\operatorname{supp}\nabla\chi\subset \QQap\setminus \QQa$ (a collar away from the bottom), a standard trace inequality on Lipschitz boxes gives
\begin{equation}
\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma^{-1}\, |\chi V_\psi|^2\, ds
\;\lesssim\;
\iint_{\QQap\setminus \QQa}
\Big(|\nabla(\chi V_\psi)|^2 + L^{-2}\,|\chi V_\psi|^2\Big)\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma.
\label{eq:trace-V}
\end{equation}
Expanding $\nabla(\chi V_\psi)=\chi\nabla V_\psi+V_\psi\nabla\chi$ and using $\|\nabla\chi\|_{L^\infty}\lesssim L^{-1}$ yields
\begin{equation}
\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma^{-1}\, |\chi V_\psi|^2\, ds
\;\lesssim\;
\iint_{\QQap} |\nabla V_\psi|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma.
\label{eq:trace-V-final}
\end{equation}
For Poisson extensions one has the exact area/trace identity
\begin{equation}
\iint_{\mathbb{R}_+^2} |\nabla V_\psi|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma
\;\asymp\; \|\psi\|_{\dot H^{1/2}(\mathbb{R})}^2,
\label{eq:LP}
\end{equation}
and the same bound holds on any Carleson box (up to an absolute constant depending only on $\alpha',\alpha$). In particular, for a fixed normalized template $\psi_{L,t_0}$, the quantity
\[
\mathcal{E}_\psi \;:=\;
\Bigg(\int_{\partial \QQap \setminus (I\times\{0\})} \sigma^{-1}\, |\chi V_\psi|^2\, ds\Bigg)^{1/2}
\]
is a scale–invariant constant.


\smallskip
Combining \eqref{eq:CS}, \eqref{eq:trace-U}, and \eqref{eq:trace-V-final} gives
\[
|R_{\mathrm{side}}|+|R_{\mathrm{top}}|
\;\lesssim\;
\Bigg(\iint_{\QQap} |\nabla U|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma\Bigg)^{\!1/2}
\cdot
\Bigg(\iint_{\QQap} |\nabla V_\psi|^2\, \sigma \, dt\, d\sigma\Bigg)^{\!1/2},
\]
which is \eqref{eq:remainder-bound} by \eqref{eq:LP}. Plug this and the bottom contribution back into \eqref{eq:green} to obtain \eqref{eq:pairing}.


\hfill$\square$


\textbf{Remarks.}
(i) If $\chi$ is chosen to vanish on the top and vertical sides of $\QQap$ (the standard Whitney cutoff), then $R_{\mathrm{side}}=R_{\mathrm{top}}=0$ and the pairing is exact.


(ii) The sign in the pairing aligns with the conventions ``boundary normal $=-\partial_\sigma$’’ and $\log J = U+iW$; then
$\partial_\sigma U(t,0)=-W'(t)$, so the bottom term is $\int_I \psi\,(-W')$.


(iii) All implicit constants depend only on $\alpha,\alpha'$ and the fixed test template; nothing depends on $I,t_0,L$ or on the particular $U$ beyond its box Dirichlet energy.

* Cayley ⇒ Schur on a set with Re F ≥ 0 (off zeros)


- Description: If Re F ≥ 0 on a set S, then Θ=(F−1)/(F+1) is Schur on S. This is the local glue used off zero sets or on rectangles.


* Standalone problem: Let F be holomorphic on a domain D and assume 0 ≤ Re F on S ⊆ D. Prove |(F−1)/(F+1)| ≤ 1 on S. If needed, assume S is a rectangle or a zero-free set where F is holomorphic and bounded.


___

### Proof


Let \( z \in S \subseteq D \), and set \( w = F(z) \). By assumption, \( F \) is holomorphic on \( D \), so \( w \) is well-defined, and \( \operatorname{Re} w \geq 0 \).


We need to show that \( \left| \frac{w - 1}{w + 1} \right| \leq 1 \).


First, note that \( w + 1 \neq 0 \): since \( \operatorname{Re} w \geq 0 \), we have \( \operatorname{Re}(w + 1) = \operatorname{Re} w + 1 \geq 1 > 0 \), so \( w + 1 \) cannot be zero (as the real part would be zero only if it were purely imaginary, but here it is at least 1).


Now, compute the squared magnitudes:


\[
|w - 1|^2 = (w - 1)(\overline{w} - 1) = |w|^2 - w - \overline{w} + 1 = |w|^2 - 2 \operatorname{Re} w + 1,
\]


\[
|w + 1|^2 = (w + 1)(\overline{w} + 1) = |w|^2 + w + \overline{w} + 1 = |w|^2 + 2 \operatorname{Re} w + 1.
\]


Subtract these:


\[
|w - 1|^2 - |w + 1|^2 = (|w|^2 - 2 \operatorname{Re} w + 1) - (|w|^2 + 2 \operatorname{Re} w + 1) = -4 \operatorname{Re} w.
\]


Since \( \operatorname{Re} w \geq 0 \), it follows that \( -4 \operatorname{Re} w \leq 0 \), so \( |w - 1|^2 \leq |w + 1|^2 \).


Assuming \( w + 1 \neq 0 \) (which we established), divide both sides by \( |w + 1|^2 > 0 \):


\[
\left| \frac{w - 1}{w + 1} \right|^2 \leq 1,
\]


hence


\[
\left| \frac{w - 1}{w + 1} \right| \leq 1.
\]


Equality holds if and only if \( \operatorname{Re} w = 0 \).


This holds pointwise for each \( z \in S \), without needing additional assumptions on \( S \) beyond \( S \subseteq D \) and the given conditions on \( F \). The holomorphicity of \( F \) ensures \( F(z) \) is defined, but the inequality itself is algebraic on the values of \( F \).


(If the context requires bounding on specific sets like rectangles or zero-free regions, this could be to apply maximum modulus principles or avoid singularities in broader arguments, but it is not necessary for this standalone inequality.)
